Nature Diaries

My Experiments Outdoor(and Indoor)

For the past few years, I have been quietly experimenting with tiny ecosystems, terrace gardens, backyard farming, terrariums, aquascapes, and small living worlds inside glass.

I am yet to formally begin documenting these journeys. Most of these years were spent experimenting, observing quietly, failing, restarting, and slowly understanding what truly draws me toward Nature and ecosystems.

But I think it is finally time to pause, look back, and begin maintaining these diaries more intentionally. This space will slowly come alive with observations —about soil,mosses,terrariums,aquascapes,tiny ecosystems,travels,patterns in Nature,and the quiet transformations I continue to witness along the way. And maybe someday, I could also document the journey of transforming a barren patch of land into a living, thriving ecosystem. Right now, that dream still feels distant.

What began nearly five years ago as a simple attempt to spend less time in front of screens and live a little closer to nature slowly opened a door into something much deeper. I started small — a terrace garden with a few grow bags, some seeds, basic soil mixes, and endless curiosity. Watching seedlings emerge, flowers turn into vegetables, and pollinators visit the garden brought a kind of joy I had never experienced before. Gardening became more than a hobby. It became a way of observing life closely.

But slowly, I realized what fascinated me more was something deeper:how life slowly organizes itself into ecosystems. The next season, the experiments expanded into small patches in my hometown farm. My husband helped prepare the soil, and we even installed a small biogas unit where kitchen waste became nutrition for the plants. I started learning about pollinators, soil quality, natural pest control, moisture retention, native greens, and regenerative farming.


The land itself became a teacher. I started noticing how different soil mix behave differently, how weeds reveal the condition of the land, how certain plants naturally attract/repel insects and birds, and how life slowly returns when the environment becomes supportive.

Motherhood also changed the way I thought about food and farming. I became more conscious about what we consume, where food comes from, and how disconnected modern life has become from the ecosystems that sustain us. But somewhere along this journey, life changed direction again.

Moving into an apartment confined my interaction with land, but interestingly, it also transformed my curiosity into something new. Unable to work with larger outdoor spaces regularly, I slowly began experimenting with terrariums and aquascapes - tiny ecosystems enclosed within glass.

What initially felt like a limitation unexpectedly became an entirely different way of observing nature. These miniature ecosystems allowed me to study ecological processes much more closely:water cycles inside closed systems,moisture regulation,moss adaptation,microbial balance,decomposition,root behavior,and the slow self-organization of living systems.

What initially felt like a limitation unexpectedly became an entirely different way of observing nature. These miniature ecosystems allowed me to study ecological processes much more closely: water cycles inside closed systems, moisture regulation, moss adaptation, microbial balance, decomposition, root behavior, and the slow self-organization of living systems.

I became fascinated by how ecosystems emerge through relationships. A piece of driftwood collected during travel could slowly become habitat.Mosses would establish themselves differently depending on humidity and airflow.Condensation inside glass could create tiny weather cycles.Microorganisms would quietly stabilize the system underneath everything visible. Some systems survived for years. Some collapsed unexpectedly. But every single experiment taught me something.

Even while travelling now, I observe landscapes differently. I notice soil textures, local flora, mosses growing on stones, ferns thriving in shaded regions, fallen wood in different stages of decay, and the subtle ecological differences between places.

Sometimes I bring back small fragments from these journeys — stones, driftwood, mosses, seeds, or tiny plants — not as decoration, but as ways to continue observing the ecosystems they belong to.

Looking back now, I feel these years were not the destination themselves, but small experiments helping me understand what truly draws me toward nature. Not perfect gardens.Not aesthetic landscapes. But the quiet transformation of spaces into living ecosystems. Perhaps someday, I may work on larger ecological spaces again. But for now, these backyard experiments, miniature forests inside glass, and observations gathered through travel have taught me something invaluable: that ecosystems are not “built.” They slowly emerge when relationships, balance, diversity, and time are allowed to work together.
