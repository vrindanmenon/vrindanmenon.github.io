# Custard Chemistry

Some learning journeys begin in laboratories.  
But this one began in a kitchen.

My son wanted to make custard, so we searched for a good eggless recipe and started mixing milk, sugar, vanilla essence, and cornflour together.

Somewhere between stirring the mixture and watching it slowly change texture, a question appeared:

**Why does custard suddenly become thick?**

That one question led us into a microscopic world hidden inside starch.

## Nature's Energy Vaults

The deeper we explored, the stranger starch became.

At first, starch sounded simple — just something hidden inside the flours sitting quietly in the kitchen cabinet.

But plants seem to treat it very differently.

Imagine you are a plant.

All day long, you absorb sunlight and convert it into sugar through photosynthesis. But sugar dissolves easily in water and moves around too freely. You need a stable way to store this energy for later.

So plants link sugars together.

When plants combine 3–10 sugar units, they form oligosaccharides. Beyond that, the chains become polysaccharides — either fibre or starch.

Yup, starch is essentially just lots of sugar molecules linked together.

These starch granules are found everywhere:
- seeds
- roots
- tubers
- grains

And inside each tiny granule lives an entire molecular architecture built mainly from two giant molecules:
- amylose
- amylopectin

Both are built from repeating glucose units.

But they behave very differently.

## The Twisted Twins

At first glance, amylose and amylopectin look like simple chains of sugar molecules connected together.

But something fascinating happens because of the way the glucose units are linked.

The glucose molecules are connected through something called **alpha-1,4 glycosidic bonds**.  
(1st carbon linked to the 4th carbon, while “alpha” refers to the orientation of the bridging oxygen.)

These bonds cannot rotate freely. Invisible physical constraints restrict their movement, forcing the chain to bend at specific angles.

And when this bending repeats over and over again, the entire chain naturally begins twisting into a helix.

Not because the molecule is “trying” to form a spiral.

But because geometry quietly emerges from the structure of the bonds themselves.

Amylose is mostly a long, flexible chain. Sometimes it coils into a helical structure capable of trapping fats and flavour molecules inside its spiral cavity.

Amylopectin behaves very differently.

Instead of remaining mostly linear, it branches repeatedly into an enormous tree-like molecular network.

One behaves almost like a coiled thread.

The other behaves like a microscopic forest.

## The Architecture of the Granule

At this point, another question appeared:

If these molecules are constantly moving, how do plants pack them into stable structures?

The answer surprised us.

The starch granule is not a random tangled mass.

It is carefully organized into alternating layers:
- amorphous regions
- crystalline regions

Inside the crystalline layers, short chains from amylopectin align beside one another and twist together into tightly packed double helices.

These helices bundle together into highly ordered structures, creating microscopic crystalline domains inside the granule.

The deeper we explored, the more starch stopped feeling like powder and started feeling like architecture.

---

## The Molecular Construction Crew

None of this architecture appears automatically.

Inside the plant cell, an entire construction crew works continuously to build and organize the starch granule.

The builders are enzymes.

Some enzymes extend chains.  
Others create branches.  
Others carefully trim away excess structures.

The process feels strangely architectural.

**Starch Synthases (SS)** act like bricklayers, adding glucose units to elongate chains.

**Starch Branching Enzymes (SBE)** behave like architects, cutting and reconnecting chains to generate branching structures.

And then come the trimmers.

**Debranching Enzymes (DBE)** remove misplaced branches so the chains can align neatly into crystalline double helices.

Without this careful trimming, the structure would never organize properly.

## The Culinary Transformation

And then we returned to the custard.

As starch heats in water, the crystalline regions begin breaking apart.

Hydrogen bonds weaken.

Water enters the granule.

The structure swells — a process called gelatinisation.

Amylose chains slowly begin leaking outward into the surrounding liquid.

At some point, the system reaches a threshold.

The chains and swollen fragments begin colliding, tangling, and connecting into a giant microscopic network spread throughout the liquid.

Physicists describe this using **percolation theory** — isolated structures suddenly linking together into a connected web.

Custard thickens because of this invisible network.

What we experience as texture is actually structure emerging at microscopic scales.

The more we explored starch, the more it stopped feeling like an ingredient and started feeling like architecture hidden inside matter.

## The Blue Secret

There is a beautiful little experiment often used to detect starch.

Add a drop of iodine solution to starch, and suddenly the mixture turns deep blue-black.

For a long time, this almost felt like magic.

But the colour appears because of geometry.

The iodine molecules slip inside the helical structure formed by amylose chains, almost like beads entering a spiral tunnel.

Once trapped inside the helix, the iodine molecules interact with the electrons along the starch structure, changing the way light is absorbed and reflected.

The result is the characteristic deep blue-black colour of the iodine test.

What fascinated me most was this:

the colour is not simply “inside” iodine or starch alone.

It emerges from the interaction between molecular structure, electron behaviour, and light itself.

A tiny microscopic spiral suddenly becomes visible to our eyes.

And somehow, a bowl of custard had quietly led us into chemistry, geometry, light, structure, and pattern.

Still exploring. Still connecting dots.
